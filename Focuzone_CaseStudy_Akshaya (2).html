01
Problem Space

Productivity tools feel cold. Mental health apps feel clinical.

Neither speaks to the whole person trying to perform and feel okay at the same time. Focuzone bridges the gap.

Fragmented tooling

Users juggle 4+ separate apps for focus timers, mood journals, and therapy booking — creating context-switching overhead that worsens the problem they're trying to solve.

Accessibility barriers

Mental health support remains out of reach due to cost, waitlists, or stigma. Digital tools must lower this barrier — not replicate it.

Clinical UX language

Most mental health apps use diagnostic or cold framing. "Log your emotional state" increases the anxiety it's trying to reduce. Language is a design decision.

No onboarding support

Empty dashboards require effort from users who are already overwhelmed. The blank-slate UX is a failure state for anyone struggling with focus or anxiety.

Key Insights

What the research revealed

01

Productivity and mental health are inseparable — the product shouldn't make users choose

Every interview surfaced the same tension: wanting to perform while struggling to feel okay. A product that treats these as separate domains will always feel incomplete.

Core product principle
02

Tone is a design system decision — clinical language actively increases anxiety

Users consistently responded better to warm, conversational language over diagnostic framing. "How are you feeling today?" outperforms "Log your emotional state."

Copywriting · Voice & Tone
03

The blank slate is a UX failure — especially for overwhelmed users

Citing "not knowing where to begin" as a bigger barrier than lack of motivation. Onboarding and session initiation must be frictionless and immediately rewarding.

Onboarding · Session UX
04

Streaks work — but only when they don't punish absence

Gamification that penalises missed days actively damages the relationship with the app. Progress mechanics must be honest, forgiving, and encouraging — not coercive.

Engagement · Habit design
Reflection

What this project taught me

Designing for mental health demands a different kind of care — for ethics, tone, and the unintended consequences of every design decision.

What I learned

  • Tone is a design system decision — every word carries emotional weight in mental health contexts
  • State design matters as much as happy-path design — break and completion states shape retention
  • Wireframes exposed layout problems that hi-fi would have buried under visual noise
  • Personas built from real interviews are fundamentally different from assumed archetypes
  • Design tokens create consistency not just visually but in the emotional rhythm of the product

What I'd explore next

  • Full accessibility audit — colour contrast, dynamic type, screen reader support
  • Longitudinal testing — does the app actually reduce anxiety over 4+ weeks?
  • Coach-side interface — the therapist experience is currently underdesigned
  • Wearable integration — mood data from Apple Watch to reduce manual check-in friction
  • Dark/light mode system — theming tokens already structured for this extension
